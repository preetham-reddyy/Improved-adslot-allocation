KDD Cup 2000 Evaluation Criteria

Updated 7/31/2000


Introduction

This document describes the questions and the evaluation criteria that were used to rate the participants in KDD Cup 2000.
Over 170 NDAs were received.  31 entries were submitted (called participants below).
A single winner was declared for each of the five questions.  One of the participants won two questions.
Honorable mentions were given to other participants that were judged as significant or very close to the winner.
Questions one and two had one honorable mention each;  Question 3 had two honorable mentions; Question 4 had three honorable mentions; and Question 5 had no honorable mention.
 
  • For questions 3,4,5, for which no simple objective measure exists, we talked to retail experts at Gazelle and at Blue Martini about the submissions. We then formalized the evaluations by collecting all of the significant insights, weighting them, and creating a score based on a linear combination of: the observations, the correctness of the submission, and the presentation.  The weights were as follows:
    • Presentation (0-10), weight 3: How readable and easy to understand is the presentation?  Are there graphs, tables, and figures that business people can understand?  Was there an effort to distill the important information, or are 37 graphs presented (one for each column with some relationship), letting the business user figure things out?
    • Correctness (0-10), weight 3: Are the claims correct (see below for incorrect statistics when using clipped data)?  Is the support (number of matches) mentioned (see above for low support of purchases from shopnow.com referrals)?
    • Bonus (0-10), weight 1: Extra insight or observations that were very interesting (but not necessarily an observation about the question).  We rarely awarded bonus points here and most participants got zero for this factor.
    • Insights (0-2), varying weights: As described below for each question, there were many insight factors that were created as the union of all insights from participants (e.g., over 30 for questions 3 and 4).  Each participant was ranked 0-2 on identifying each factor.


    Many observations were given low weight (sometimes zero) because they simply correlate with other more fundamental ones.
    For example, it is true (and not well known hence interesting) that your palm size correlates with your life expectancy: the larger your palm, the less you will live, on average.  While this is very interesting at first, once you factor the fact that females have smaller palms (females live about 6 years longer than males in the U.S.), the effect of palm size in the two populations disappears and the insight itself seems much less interesting.
    A similar observation is that shoe size correlates with reading ability, yet this is obvious once you factor in age: small children do not read as well as teenagers.

    In our web mining data, many seemingly interesting observations are obvious once you discover the underlying cause, usually time or session length.  Peeling the onion to find the underlying cause of many observations is something many participants have failed to do.  We spent significant time peeling the onion ourselves on some of the "interesting observations," only to find out there was nothing left once you took time or session length into account.  Some examples

    • SendEmail indicates is a registration form question where users are asked whether they would like Gazelle to send them e-mail.

      • Many participants noted the correlation between this and other factors, such as being a "heavy" spender (question 3).  However, few participants plotted the response to this question over time to discover that it varied dramatically in time.  We asked Gazelle about this and they mentioned that the default was changed from yes to no around early March (looks like 2/29) and that it was changed again afterwards.  This is a great example of something that was not conveyed to us (and therefore the participants) early, yet some participants discovered it. click to see
    • Several other attributes from the initial registration form that were not used in the second registration form are also simply predictors of time.
    • Only a few participants noted that Donna Karan wasn't available on the site prior to 2/26! click to see
    • Many participants noticed that referrers had significant effects, but referrers are unstable over time. Some referrers lasted for very short periods (e.g., mycoupons) and some started late (winnie-cooper) click to see.
    • Some participants forgot that high percentage is meaningless if the support is small.  For example, some participants mentioned that all (100%) of customers referred by FashionMall.com and ShopNow.com were "heavy spenders" for question 3, but did not realize that the actual number of purchases was extremely small: 17 and 6.

    An interesting point is how the conversion rates differ (we ignore winnie-cooper visitors who may be looking for pictures more than actual items to purchase).  The ratio of conversion rates between mycoupons.com and shopnow.com is 207!  Of course (as seen below in question 3), the buyers from mycoupons are usually low spenders, but their contribution over time needs to be assessed.  Acquisition costs could go as high as $200 per customer, so spending $10 on a promotion might certainly be useful if the person might come back later and spend more money without the coupon.
    The most common mistake for questions 4 and 5 was reporting statistics based on the aggregated training data without filtering out the clipped sessions.  Because the data consists of two populations, one of which (the continue label) is clipped (or right censored in statistics terminology), computations that depend on the sessions being complete are incorrect.  Participants should have filtered out the continue/clipped sessions that are marked and generated statistics from the rest of the data, which represents the actual distributions.  The best example is the probability of exiting after k pages, which Jorge from e-steam highlighted.  Participants that computed the statistics based on the training set (ratio of continue to leave) had dramatically wrong statistics.  For example, several reported 87.33% for exiting at the first page while the true number is 61.35%.  Furthermore, the whole trend for abandonment is incorrect: based on the training data label, it seems that the probability of leaving the site increases initially but the real calculation shows that it is decreasing.  Click to see.
    A related mistake was to count visitors using the training set in question 3.  These are only visitors that purchased something.

    Questions and Evaluation Criteria

    Questions 1: Given a set of page views, will the visitor view another page on the site or will the visitor leave?
    Motivation: the ability to predict whether a visitor will leave can help determine the best page to display (e.g., there may be three alternative pages a site can display in response to a given link and each can be evaluated on the likelihood that it will cause the user to leave) or action can be taken to encourage the visitor to stay (e.g., special promotion).

    Evaluation criteria: the ratio of correct predictions on the test set (0-1 loss).
    The winner was the participant with the highest accuracy.
    We looked at the accuracy on non-trivial sessions (5 or more clicks) and awarded an honorable mention to the participant with the highest accuracy, who happened to have the second highest accuracy for all sessions.
     

    Questions 2: Given a set of page views, which product brand (Hanes, Donna Karan, American Essentials, on none) will the visitor view in the remainder of the session?
    Motivation: If you could put a single dynamic link (e.g., picture and URL) on the current page, pointing to a brand, which brand would you choose?  To make the problem manageable, we are asking you to predict one of three common brands, or other.
    Evaluation criteria:  score on the test set determined as follows: 2 units if the session visited the predicted brand, 1 unit if the session did not visit any of the three brands and the prediction was none; 0 otherwise.
    The winner was the participant with the highest score and the second highest score for non-trivial sessions (5 or more clicks).
    The honorable mention went to the participant with the second highest score who had the highest score on the non-trivial sessions.
